# Problem Definition
## Boise Public Works Infrastructure Prioritization System (PWIS)

**Document Type:** Strategic Problem Framing
**Domain:** Wastewater/Sewer Collection, Geothermal District Heating, Pressurized Irrigation

---

## The Problem

Boise Public Works manages three distinct utility systems with aging infrastructure requiring coordinated capital investment:

1. **Wastewater/Sewer Collection:** ~900 miles of pipe (4,200 segments), 28 lift stations, 2 Water Reclamation Facilities
2. **Geothermal District Heating:** 20+ miles of pipeline, 177°F circulation, ~90 downtown buildings, 3 production wells + injection well (one of largest municipal geothermal systems in US)
3. **Pressurized Irrigation (PI):** 14 subdivisions, seasonal Apr 15–Oct 15 operation, Boise River source

Much of the sewer infrastructure was installed between 1940 and 1980 and is approaching or exceeding its expected service life. Geothermal and PI systems also face aging components and efficiency degradation.

Without a data-driven prioritization system, capital investment decisions for pipe replacement and rehabilitation are made based on emergency response patterns, political pressure, or institutional memory. This leads to:

1. **Reactive spending:** Emergency repairs cost 3–5x more than planned replacements
2. **Inequitable investment:** Districts with the loudest complaints get funding, not those with the worst infrastructure
3. **Regulatory risk:** Sanitary sewer overflows (SSOs), wastewater treatment violations, inadequate geothermal capacity
4. **Budget inefficiency:** CIP dollars spent on low-priority assets while critical pipes deteriorate
5. **System interdependencies ignored:** Decisions in one system (e.g., PI timing) impact others (e.g., sewer I&I during irrigation season)

## The Solution

PWIS provides a systematic, transparent, and auditable framework for utility infrastructure investment prioritization. It scores every pipe segment on six factors (condition, break history, capacity, criticality, material risk, age), produces tiered action recommendations, and runs scenario analyses to support budget planning.

## Key Stakeholders

- **Public Works Director** — Uses priority tiers and scenario analysis across three systems for unified CIP budget presentations to Council
- **Sewer Division Supervisor** — Uses district-level sewer rankings to plan crew deployment, prioritize SSO risk mitigation
- **Geothermal System Manager** — Uses heat exchanger and well condition scores to plan retrofits and injection well maintenance
- **PI Operations Supervisor** — Uses seasonal demand and pressure data to plan PI main/lateral replacements before irrigation season
- **Finance Department** — Uses deferral cost analysis to justify multi-year CIP funding requests across sewer/geothermal/PI
- **City Council** — Reviews executive dashboard comparing investment needs across all three systems
- **Regulatory Compliance (EPA/DEQ)** — Uses sewer condition and capacity data for CMOM permit reporting; geothermal efficiency data for sustainability targets

## Success Metrics

**Sewer System:**
- Reduction in sewer overflow incidents (target: 15% in Year 1)
- Reduction in emergency repair rate (target: 20% decrease in Year 1)
- Increase in planned vs. reactive maintenance ratio (target: 70/30 planned/reactive)
- All Critical-tier sewer pipes addressed within 2 CIP cycles

**Geothermal System:**
- Maintain 177°F circulation temperature (downtime reduction by 10%)
- Well performance sustaining 350+ gpm from production wells
- Injection well capacity optimized for Year-round operation

**Pressurized Irrigation:**
- Zero pressure failures during peak irrigation season (Apr 15–Oct 15)
- Service availability >98% during seasonal operation
- CIP budget utilization above 90% (vs. current pattern of underspending on planned work)

**Cross-System:**
- Equitable distribution: no district receives >40% of annual budget
- Unified CIP dashboard adopted by Council for comparative system investment decisions
